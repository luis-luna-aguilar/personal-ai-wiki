# AI is Everywhere. The Agentic Organization Isn't Yet.

McKinsey Podcast transcript — Alexis Krivkovich (Senior Partner, McKinsey Bay Area) with Lucia Rahilly (Global Editorial Director). Edited for clarity and length.

## The great AI paradox

More than 80 percent of companies say they're not yet seeing impact on the bottom line from AI investments. The real question is "How do we meet this moment and create the agentic organization of the future?" Leaders are asking: How are roles going to change? What are the skills we'll need? How do I bring along our workforce with excitement, not fear? How do I drive that change so it hits every corner of the organization?

## Business model implications

Near-zero marginal cost of delivery changes what companies can offer. If a customer has an agent that can move money frictionlessly across bank accounts to seek the best rate, the entirety of the traditional financial services moat changes. Hypersegmentation down to the unit of one becomes possible. On the receiving end: agents interacting on behalf of buyers fundamentally restructures competitive dynamics.

## Rethinking teams for the agentic era

The day-to-day workflows and the rituals around ways of working will need to fundamentally change — that is what "operating model shift" means in practice: how the hours of the day happen differently, the process of overseeing an agent population, how you engage in problem-solving as a team, with the right governance and risk controls.

Org chart impacts vary by function:
- **Pharma R&D**: huge squads of agents accelerating innovation — scientists not replaced but supercharged
- **HR, finance, legal** (cost centers): real hope for transformation of who does the work and enabling more capacity elsewhere
- **Management layers**: most companies have added 1–3 extra layers over the past decade; AI may enable leaders to manage broader scopes, allowing structure to flatten

**75% of roles need fundamental reshaping right now.** Nearly half of leaders say they see skill gaps. Most would benefit from more training and capability building all the way to senior leadership.

## What 'reimagining workflows' really means

The best use cases enabling scalable impact are where a workflow is reimagined in its entirety — not point solutions. Examples: insurance underwriting end-to-end; HR "hire to onboard" end-to-end.

End-to-end workflow redesign requires systems thinking: where does it benefit from an agent? Where do I need a human in the loop, or above the loop, to supervise? Where do I need a team of agents? How do I make them reusable?

Team composition for process redesign needs people at all levels — both leaders and employees deep in day-to-day flows. AI enables executive function — quality control, review, pattern recognition assessment — to be done through the agentic process. Humans provide judgment on top.

Two models for tech function organization: central SWAT teams, or embedded expertise inside each business area.

## Humans above the loop vs. in the loop

**In the loop**: agents do pieces of the process, then pass to a human who does other pieces.

**Above the loop**: teams of AI agents do most or the entire core process; the human's role becomes judgment on top.

**Real example — American Arbitration Association**: Traditional process involves gathering hundreds/thousands of data points (contracts, email exhibits), reviewing the case file, deciding based on agreement terms. This takes a long time. McKinsey trained agents on closed case files to build the timeline, review the fact base, look at both sides, come to a summary decision. Agents could do much of the core work — in some cases, better. A human still reviews the whole process and asks "Do I agree?" The judgment layer remains critical, but the work underneath is done end to end by agentic AI.

## Leadership requirements

Leaders need to show up differently: not dabbling (tightening an email here, querying there) but radically changing how they spend their time and being transparent about it to the org.

A trust gap exists: hallucinations are real, AI slop creates more work rather than less. Leaders need to balance excitement about what's possible with good judgment and risk management while acknowledging "we're in learning mode."

## Skills in transition

Skills being *affected*: research, math, data science.

Skills *rising in importance*: strategic thinking, systems orientation, people management, oversight, judgment, problem-solving, operational leadership above the loop.

## The junior talent problem

If you eliminate entry-level work (grunt work through which pattern recognition is built), you eventually run out of senior talent. Junior employees won't have 20 years of pattern recognition but also won't face the hurdle of disrupting an established way of working. Learning and development should be at the *center* of the employee journey, not a periodic sidecar.

## Culture and change management

Change management is no longer episodic — it's a perpetual state. Organizations that foster curiosity and continuous learning will have an advantage. Need people who can do joint problem-solving across the org, coach teams into new models with optimism. Critical balance: take risks without betting the company. Two-way doors, not one-way doors.
