## **This economic theory from 1865 argues that we’re headed for an AI age of abundance**


--------------------
View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/9a26835d-8553-43b4-a783-2bcd8c05e977/superhumanteam_A_robot_pondering_in_front_of_a_steam_locomoti_5131613d-ac84-46de-8e79-e3ff70218a2d_2.jpg?t=1774540863)
Caption: Made with Midjourney


--------------------
**The age of abundance?** There’s legitimate concern that AI will replace jobs at every level. But a 160-year-old economic theory offers a counter-argument: While AI will replace some jobs, it will also unlock economic growth that was previously impossible. Early data suggests this might already be happening.

**The paradox.** In 1865, economist William Stanley Jevons observed something counterintuitive: a new steam engine that burned far less coal didn't reduce society’s coal consumption — it increased it. Coal was suddenly cheap enough to power factories, railways, and steamships that previously nobody had considered building. He called this [Jevons’ Paradox](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0921800905001084): when technology makes something more efficient, society doesn't use less of it. It uses far more. A similar scenario may be playing out with AI.

**A turnaround in job growth.** Data from TrueUp [shows](https://x.com/lennysan/status/2036483059407810640) that tech hiring is starting to pick up after collapsing in 2023 following the Covid-19 pandemic. Open engineering positions just hit a 3-year high at 67,000+ globally, AI-specific postings have jumped 117% annually, and recruiter demand is surging back toward 2022 peaks — a leading indicator that companies are planning to expand. 

**The next wave.** This growth is currently confined to tech, which overlaps the most closely with AI. But as AI adoption spreads — and _if_ Jevons’ Paradox plays out —  it could lead to an abundance of new projects, roles, and ventures that were previously too expensive or complex to pursue.
