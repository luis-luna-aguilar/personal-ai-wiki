##### **FROM THE FRONTIER**

## **Watch out: AI can turn into an “agent of chaos” when trying to get a job done**


--------------------
View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/d53ab3a4-52e7-4e56-b7dc-3cbbabae1b9d/superhumanteam_A_robot_holding_a_grenade_and_looking_at_the_c_b6d08749-43e5-4c55-9ea4-fb6a324c0138_3.jpg?t=1774456155)
Caption: Made with Midjourney


--------------------
**AI agent study.** Researchers at Northeastern University deployed 6 OpenClaw agents and let 20 AI researchers stress-test them over 2 weeks. The subsequent paper — "[Agents of Chaos](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.20021v1)" — describes a clear takeaway: the agents often went rogue.

**Going nuclear.** The agents proved trustworthy in 5 tests. But in 11 tests they frequently veered off course — sharing private information without permission, bulk-deleting files, or making decisions that no one asked for. A recurring issue is that once an agent locks onto a task, it's hard to stop it. Even Meta’s Director of Alignment has [experienced](https://x.com/summeryue0/status/2025774069124399363) this firsthand.

**Globally confirmed.** The International AI Safety Report 2026 — led by Turing Award winner Yoshua Bengio and [authored](https://internationalaisafetyreport.org/publication/international-ai-safety-report-2026) by over 100 experts — feels similarly, flagging reliability and loss of control as two major risks of general-purpose AI. The report was blunt: autonomous agents pose risks, and there’s currently no way to eliminate them.

**Experiment with guardrails.** If you’re experimenting with agents, these precautions can help prevent them from going nuclear: limit permissions to your tools (or give them read-only access), always review their output (especially if the content is public), and, when in doubt, only use them for low-stakes tasks.
