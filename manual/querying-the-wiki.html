Asking questions

Once the wiki has content in it, you can ask the LLM questions and get answers grounded in the pages you've ingested. Answers come with a confidence label based on how fresh the underlying pages are.

Two modes

Wiki-only: ask

Default mode. The LLM answers strictly from the wiki, reading only the pages relevant to your question. It reports confidence based on the as_of dates of the pages it used.

  • "ask: what's the best tool for agentic coding right now?"
  • "based on the wiki, which models lead on tool use?"
  • "how does Cursor compare to Claude Code?"

Wiki + web: ask-verify

Answers from the wiki first, then uses the browser-based fetcher to check the answer against fresh web content. Reports where the wiki agrees, disagrees, or is behind. Proposes a dry-run update if anything new turned up.

  • "ask-verify: is Cursor still leading SWE-bench?"
  • "verify against the web: what's the latest frontier model benchmark?"

For ask-verify, it helps if you provide URLs to check against. If you don't, the LLM will tell you what URLs would be useful.

Confidence labels

Every answer starts with a confidence header like:

**Confidence:** high — newest relevant page as of 2026-04-09 (2 days old, threshold 14d).

The bands:

LabelMeaningWhen
high Trust the answer. Pages are within their freshness window. age ≤ threshold
medium Answer may be outdated. Consider verifying. threshold < age ≤ 2× threshold
stale Strong warning. Don't trust without checking the web. age > 2× threshold

Thresholds are per-page-type and configurable in config.yml → stale_thresholds_days. Defaults:

  • state-of: 14 days
  • model: 7 days
  • tool: 30 days
  • benchmark: 14 days
  • workflow: 60 days
  • concept: 90 days

What the LLM does to answer

  1. Reads wiki/index.md.
  2. Identifies the relevant state-of page(s) and 2–5 specific tool/model/benchmark pages.
  3. Reads only those pages — it does not walk the whole tree.
  4. Synthesizes an answer with wikilinks to the sources.
  5. Reports confidence based on the oldest relevant page's as_of.

This is why answers are cheap even as the wiki grows — the index is the router, and reads are targeted.

Asking about history

By default the LLM does not read wiki/history/ — it stays focused on current state. If you want historical context, say so:

  • "how has Cursor evolved over the last 3 months?"
  • "what was the top coding model in February?"
  • "trace the benchmark trajectory of SWE-bench leaders"

For these, the LLM will open the relevant history files in addition to the current page.

Filing good answers back into the wiki

If an answer turned out to be a good analysis or comparison, you can save it as a new wiki page:

  • "file that comparison into the wiki"
  • "save that as a concept page"

This triggers a normal proposal flow — the answer becomes a draft you can review and apply.

Output formats

You can ask for different response shapes:

  • "make a comparison table of the top 4 IDE coding tools"
  • "draft a short brief on the state of agents for a non-technical reader"
  • "generate a markdown report I can paste into a document"

If the LLM produces something worth keeping, ask for it to be filed.

When the wiki doesn't know

A good answer includes honest "I don't know" or "the wiki is silent on this." The LLM won't hallucinate — if the wiki lacks data on something, it'll say so and suggest what sources would fill the gap.

Two useful follow-ups:

  • "find me sources to fill that gap" — the LLM suggests search queries and URLs you could feed into fetch_url.py.
  • "ask-verify" — the same question but with a web check on top.
